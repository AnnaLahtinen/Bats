Fun Facts

  • Bats are the only true flying mammals in the world
  • A tiny pipistrelle can eat up to 3,000 insects in a night!
  • Without bats you wouldn't have for example dates, vanilla, bananas, breadfruit, guavas, avocados, mangoes, Tequila or chewing gum.
  • Bats are more closely related to humans than they are to mice.
  • The majority of the world's bats eat insects. In the tropics bats also eat foods like fruit, flowers, frogs, fish, blood, even other bats.
  • Bats can eat up to 1,200 mosquitoes an hour!
  • Pallid bats eat scorpions!
  • Bat droppings, called guano, are one of the richest fertilizers.
  • The world's largest bat is the “flying fox,” which lives on islands in the South Pacific. A full-grown adult can have a wingspan of 167.6 cm and weigh up to 1.2 kg.
  • The world's smallest bat is the Kitti's Hog-nosed bat, also called the bumblebee bat, which is found in Thailand. They are 29-33mm long, weight about 1.7-2.0g and have a wingspan of 13-14.5cm.
  • Baby bats are called pups! Most bats have only one pup a year.
  • Bats have few natural predators — disease is one of the biggest threats.
  • The longest-living bat is 41 years old.
  • Like cats, bats clean themselves.
  • Bats have bellybuttons!
  • Ancient civilizations worshipped bat-like gods.
  • Blood-sucking 'Vampire bats' are real.
  • When in flight, bats hearts beat 1,000 times a minute!
  • Bats are hugely important for people and the planet.
  • Currently, the oldest known fossil bat is 50 million years old.
  • Bats are NOT blind; their eyes are attuned to low-light conditions and some species can see color.
  • Most but not all species of bats are nocturnal (active at night).
  • Some bats live solitary lives, getting together only to mate, but a majority of species gather together in colonies of hundreds to thousands of individuals.
  • Bats do not spend any energy hanging upside down by their feet.  In the relaxed position, the toes and claws on a bat's foot are curled closed and muscles have to be used to open them.
  • The wings of a bat are formed by a thin membrane that runs along the length of their arm, is stretched between their long, thin fingers, attached to their hind legs, and often a tail.
  • Bat Colonies Save Humans Billions in Pest Control.
